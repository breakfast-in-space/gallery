Breakfast in Space is a multidisciplinary creative practice working across photography, music production, and visual art. This gallery collects work made in the margins — images, sounds, and objects that exist somewhere between intention and chance, between the studio and the world outside it.

The work gathered here resists easy categorization. Photography bleeds into visual art; studio process becomes its own kind of document. What connects it is an attention to the texture of experience — the way light behaves in a room, the way silence carries weight, the way material pushes back. Each collection is a record of a specific way of paying attention.

Collection I

Photography

On Photography

A study in light, shadow, and the spaces between. Each frame a moment suspended — brief, still, irreversible. These images don't try to explain themselves. They ask only to be looked at.

Shot across locations and years, the photographs are unified not by subject but by a shared quality of attention — a willingness to stay with a thing until something shifts.

"The photograph is not what was photographed, it is something else — a new and reduced version of the visible world."

Collection II

Music

On Music

Sound made visible. The architecture of rhythm and resonance, captured in still form. These images document the conditions under which music is made — the rooms, the equipment, the particular quality of light at certain hours.

The studio as a place: not a neutral space but one that shapes what gets made inside it. What you hear in the music begins, in some way, here.

Collection III

Visual Art

On Visual Art

Form, texture, the language of gesture. Work that asks to be looked at slowly — resisting the speed of everything else. These pieces don't illustrate ideas so much as embody them: in their material, their scale, their resistance to easy reading.

Each piece begins with a question. The work ends when the question dissolves into something quieter — not an answer, but a different kind of attention.

Production

On Production

Behind the work, the work. Process as practice — the studio as a living document of becoming. These images record the conditions of making: the tools, the surfaces, the half-finished states that rarely survive into the final work.

The gap between intention and execution is where the real work lives. This is an attempt to make that gap visible.
